Swimming Techniques

Welcome to Silver! You’ve already got the engine and the drive, but moving up to the next level isn't just about out-muscling the water anymore—it’s about out-smarting it. In this group, we’re shifting gears from just surviving the yardage to really dialing in how your body moves through the lane. If we can clean up your technique and get rid of the bad habits now, you’re going to drop serious time and make everything feel twice as easy. Let’s look at exactly what you need to focus on for each stroke.

Fly is tough, but once you find the rhythm, it feels awesome. The movement has to start in your chest and core, creating a smooth wave that travels all the way down to your toes. Remember, you get exactly two kicks for every one arm pull: the first one hits when your hands splash into the water, and the second one snaps down right as your hands finish pushing past your thighs. When you bring your arms back around, swing them low and wide right over the surface, and keep your chin skimming right along the top of the water.

Backstroke is a game of rotation, but the trick is keeping your head completely still. Your shoulders should roll side to side with every single pull, but your chin stays up and your eyes stay locked on the sky. When your hand comes over your head, make sure it enters pinky-first so you can grab the water immediately without slipping. Underneath the surface, bend your elbow to push the water straight back toward the wall behind you. Finally, stop bending your knees so much on the kick—that just acts like a brake. Keep your legs straight and make the top of the water boil.

Breaststroke is all about timing and patience, and the rhythm never changes: Pull, Breathe, Kick, Glide. Keep your pull tight and narrow—sweep your hands out and snap them back together right under your chin, never letting them swim past your shoulders. As you pop up to grab a quick breath, instantly drive your hips forward. For the kick, snap your heels to your butt, turn your toes out like a frog, and whip those legs together. The absolute secret here is holding a rock-solid streamline for a quick second at the end of the kick to ride that free speed before you start the next stroke.

With free, you want to think about staying as long and flat as possible. The second you lift your eyes to see where you're going, your hips drop like an anchor and you're fighting a wall of water. Keep your head down, looking right at that black line. When your hand reaches out in front, make sure you aren't crossing over the middle of your face—keep your lanes wide. Drop your fingertips early to lock onto the water with your whole forearm, roll your hips to power the stroke, and keep that kick small, fast, and steady right from your hips.
